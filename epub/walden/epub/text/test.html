"That's me. Have a drink?" "I'll have a lemon squash if you don't mind. Can't drink in the middle of the day." The Indian rose from his table and approached with deference. "You remember me, Mr. Harris. Perhaps you would tell your friend, Mr. Harris, of my talents. Perhaps he would like to read my letters of recommendation ..." The grubby sheaf of envelopes was always in his hand. "The leaders of society." "Go off. Beat it, you old scoundrel," Harris said. "How did you know my name?" Wilson asked. "Saw it on a cable. I'm a cable censor," Harris said. "What a job. What a place." "I can see from here, Mr. Harris, that your fortune has changed considerably. If you would step with me for a moment into the bathroom . . ." "Beat it, Gunga Din." "Why the bathroom?" Wilson asked. "He always tells fortunes there. I suppose it's the only private room available. I never thought of asking why." "Been here long?" "Eighteen bloody months." "Going home soon?" Harris stared over the tin roofs towards the harbour. He said, "The ships all go the wrong way. But when I do get home you'll never see me here again." He lowered his voice and said with venom over his lemon squash, "I hate the place. I hate the people. I hate the bloody niggers. Mustn't call 'em that, you know."
